## #0 DPO -- Why does the ocean look blue?
```
It's because it absorbs all other colors (especially red) and reflects the blue back at us. Water molecules absorb light of various wavelengths depending on what they're made out of; water is H2O so there are two hydrogen atoms for every oxygen atom. The energy levels that electrons can have when bound into these molecules allow them to absorb photons of specific frequencies. It turns out that one of those frequencies is right around where we see blue as our eyes evolved. So the oceans reflect blue light but absorb most of everything else.
```

## #1 DPO -- Can you explain how vaccines work?
```
Vaccines work by teaching your body's immune system to recognize certain foreign substances (called antigens) as harmful so that it can respond quickly if they ever come back again. This is done through the use of weakened or dead pathogens, or their toxins, which stimulate your immune system but don't cause disease themselves. The immune response to these harmless agents will be much faster and stronger than the response against the same pathogen when it actually invades your body.
```

## #2 DPO -- What causes the seasons to change?
```
The Earth's axis is tilted 23 degrees from its orbit around the sun. The Northern Hemisphere is tilted toward the sun during summer (around June), so it receives more direct sunlight and gets warmer. In December, the North Pole points away from the Sun while the South Pole points towards it. When one pole tilts away from our star, that half of the planet experiences winter because there is less direct sunlight reaching them.

In other words, when the northern hemisphere is pointed at the sun for six months we have summer, then we point away and get winter for six month. Then the earth rotates so the southern hemisphere points directly at the Sun for six more months...summer again! And then back to pointing away...winter. This pattern repeats over the course of a year.

It should also be noted that the seasons do not exactly occur on March 1st/June 15th/September 10th/December 21st as they did in ancient times. This is due to precession which shifts the position of the equinoxes slowly each century.
```

## #3 DPO -- Explain what compound interest is and why it matters.
```
It's like interest on your bank account: if you have 10k in there at 5% annual interest (this is a pretty good rate for a regular savings account), after one year that will grow by 500 dollars (that's called "interest"). If you leave those money in there another year, you'll get 525 dollars interest because now your balance is higher (1050o) so your interest grows by 1/2 percent of that amount instead of just 1%. That means after two years you have $11,025 total. After three years you'd have ~$11638. So every year you're getting more interest not only off your original investment but also from the interest itself! This is really important when talking about retirement accounts and other investments where the idea is that you can take advantage of this effect over time. It's also important when paying off debt because it means you pay more interest each month as well.
```

## #5 DPO -- How does the internet send a message from my computer t
```
First it needs to find out where that other machine is on the network (assuming it's not on your local network). It can do this by querying for its IP address via DNS if it knows what name the other machine has, or by asking a router / gateway to forward the request using NAT (Network Address Translation) - this allows multiple computers on the same network to have their own unique IP addresses without requiring all of them to be globally unique ones.

Once it knows where to send the data, it breaks up the information into chunks which can fit through the internet (or at least the first few hops). The packets get sent off onto the internet, and as they pass along various routers they are stamped with new "next hop" information so that when they reach their destination, they know how to get there. Some routers also keep track of which source IP each packet came from, so they can forward it back correctly. When the data reaches the intended recipient, it gets reassembled in order and then forwarded to whatever program requested it originally (usually some sort of web browser).
```

## #7 DPO -- What is the difference between weather and climate?
```
Weather refers to conditions of temperature, precipitation, wind speed/direction, etc., that occur over short periods (hours, days). Climate refers to long-term averages of those same conditions; usually averaged out for 30 years at least. For example, we say it's been raining heavily every day this week--that's weather. But if I said "it rains heavily here every year on January 17th," then I'd be talking about climate. Weather can change from hour to hour, but climate changes slowly over decades or centuries.
```

## #8 DPO -- How do noise-cancelling headphones work?
```
They work by using a small speaker that plays back sound waves exactly opposite of what is coming into your ears from outside. This means they are playing the same sound but inverted (inverted = 180 degrees). When these two meet, they cancel each other out. For example if there's loud music coming through your earbuds, it will play the exact same thing but upside down so when both sounds meet together they cancel one another out completely. The problem here is that this only works for sounds that can be played back perfectly, which isn't always possible because of the fact that some noises aren't easily duplicated electronically. So these types of headphones don't always cancel all the noise completely, especially at higher frequencies where sound waves become smaller and more difficult to duplicate electronically.
```

## #9 DPO -- Why does metal feel colder than wood at the same temper
```
The difference is how quickly heat transfers from your skin to the object. Metals conduct heat much better than most organic materials (wood). When you touch something cold, heat flows from your body into that object until both reach equilibrium temperature.

If you hold a piece of copper for 10 seconds it will feel colder because it has drawn more heat away from your hand in those 1 second. Wood draws less heat away so if you hold on to it for the same amount of time it will not have cooled as much and thus feels warmer.
```

## #10 DPO -- Can you explain what a black hole is without the heavy 
```
Imagine that gravity works like this: every object has some "pull" on nearby objects. For example, Earth pulls us down because of its mass. The pull gets weaker as you move away from it, but if there's nothing else around you, eventually you'll fall into the ground (or hit something). Now imagine that instead of being pulled by one massive object, you're pulled by *all* the mass in the universe at once. It turns out that the gravitational effect of all that mass could be concentrated into a tiny volume near the center of our galaxy. If you fell into that volume, your gravitational pull would get stronger and stronger until there'd be no way for you to escape. This is what we call a black-hole.
```

## #11 DPO -- Why do onions make you cry when you cut them?
```
They contain enzymes that break down certain sulfur-containing amino acids into compounds called propanethial-S-oxide, which is irritating to your eyes (and can cause temporary blindness). It's not toxic, but it does irritate. This is also why people who work around onions should wear goggles. Onions are rich sources of these sulfur-containing compounds, so cutting onions releases more of those compounds by breaking up their cells.

It also has something to do with your tears having a higher pH than onion juice, making it easier for the acidified solution to burn the surface of the eye, and causing a reflex response where you keep crying until all the onion juice evaporates from your eye.
```

## #13 DPO -- A friend told me lightning never strikes the same place
```
Lightning is attracted to tall objects like trees, poles, towers, etc., but it doesn't mean that lightning will not strike them again and again. It's possible for a tree that has been struck by lightning once to be hit again and then again...and so on.

There have been cases where lightning rods were installed after several strikes had already occurred on nearby buildings. In these situations, the number of strikes often increases after the installation of the rod because the building was previously attracting the lightning from nearby taller structures (trees, poles) before the rod was put up. 

If you want to know more about this phenomenon, I highly recommend reading *The Lightning Strikes Twice* by Dr. Thomas A. Gaffney. This book explains how lightning rods work and why they sometimes increase the number times that lightning strikes a particular location.
```
